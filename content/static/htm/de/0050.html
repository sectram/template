Information owner

All employees who create information in their assigned area are responsible for compliance with information security within their scope of responsibility as information owners. As part of this responsibility, they are required to monitor and implement appropriate measures to protect the information they create.

The information owner is responsible for the following tasks:

  • Granting access authorizations for information
  • Define availability requirements
  • Implementing content changes
  • Informing the entitled group of persons about protective measures

Information processor

The information processor acts on behalf of the owner of the information in order to process information in daily operations or to pass it on in the necessary context. In the case of incoming information, he/she is the first information processor to determine the classification according to defined instructions of the information owner or to mark the information accordingly.

Deviations from valid instructions must be coordinated with the information owner.

Many companies use three risk classifications for sensitive data:

  • public,
  • internal,

C1 - Public

Documents classified as public: Information classified as publicly available is data that is either already generally available or intended for distribution outside an organization. It can be circulated without risk. Good examples include information intended for public use, such as press releases, job vacancies and, sales brochures.

C2 – Internal

Documents classified as "internal": This classification applies to all information that is used in business processes. Its unauthorized distribution, alteration, or damage does not result in significant damage to the involved company, its customers, employees, or partners.

Examples are all documents and data that are routinely used within a company, such as internal guidelines or telephone lists.

C3 – Confidential

Documents classified as confidential: When information is classified as confidential, its unintentional disclosure, alteration, or damage usually results in serious harm to a company, its customers, employees, or partners.

Confidential information includes, but is not limited to, intellectual property, contract negotiations, personal and health data, financial information, and account information of customers and employees.

C4 – Secret

Documents classified as secret: In some organizations, there is a further level of information classification known as "secret" or "strictly confidential." It refers to any data whose unauthorized disclosure, alteration, or damage could result in devastating harm to an organization.

For healthcare companies, this may include records of patients' mental health, sexually transmitted diseases, HIV testing, or drug abuse. Other examples include documents relating to mergers, strategic planning, or litigation.
